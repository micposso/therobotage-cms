Most organisations approach robot deployment as a technology problem. They evaluate platforms, run procurement, negotiate contracts, and then hand the robot to operations and wait. What happens next is usually instructive — and rarely what anyone planned.

The facilities that get deployment right do something different. They treat readiness as a design problem, not a logistics one. The difference is visible before the robot ever arrives.

## Three things the successful sites had in common

We observed twelve deployment sites over six months across logistics, hospitality, and healthcare environments. Three of them showed measurably better outcomes across every metric we tracked: lower escalation rates, faster staff adoption, and fewer service interruptions in the first 90 days. Here is what set them apart.

### 1. They mapped the exception before they mapped the workflow

Every site knew what the robot was supposed to do. The three high-performing sites also knew — in detail — what would happen when it stopped doing it. Who gets notified? By what channel? Who has authority to override? Who resets the system and by what procedure?

These questions had written answers before go-live. At the lower-performing sites, the answers were improvised in the moment. The improvisation showed.

![A facilities manager walks through a pre-deployment exception map with her team at a logistics site in Ohio](/images/news/readiness-exception-map.jpg)

### 2. Staff had a vocabulary before they had a robot

Frontline workers who can name what a robot is doing — even in broad terms — respond to problems differently than those who cannot. At the three high-performing sites, all staff had completed at minimum a half-day orientation that gave them language for the system's core behaviours: what the robot was sensing, why it paused, what a fault state looked like versus an edge case.

> "You can't report what you can't describe. The orientation gave people words. The words changed what they noticed and what they escalated."
>
> — Operations lead, hospitality group, Chicago

This is not a technical training. It is a literacy intervention. The goal is not to make workers understand how the robot works at an engineering level. It is to give them enough shared language to communicate about it clearly.

### 3. Feedback had a home

At most sites, frontline observations about robot behaviour have no structured destination. Workers notice things — unusual pauses, unexpected routes, interactions that felt off — but have no clear channel for surfacing them. The observations dissipate.

The three high-performing sites each had a simple, low-friction mechanism for collecting frontline feedback: a shared log, a weekly 10-minute stand-up, a designated contact point. The mechanism was less important than the fact that it existed and was visibly used by management.

## What this means for your deployment

Readiness is not a checklist item you complete before go-live. It is a condition you design for — in your staff orientation, your exception protocols, your feedback architecture. The technology is the easy part.
